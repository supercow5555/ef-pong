Backend walkthrough — Step 5 of 7: the auth path

From “pick your name” to EF SSO

The PRD deferred login on purpose — ship value first, tighten trust later. This step shows the exact road from today's honour-system identity to full company sign-in, and why every stop on it is additive: the screens and the seven tables never get rebuilt, we only change how we're sure who you are.

1The one hinge everything turns on

“Who are you?” comes up in exactly three places in EF Pong: who logged a match (entered_by), who wrote a comment (author_id), and — later — who is allowed to log a match at all. All three already point at a player row. Login never changes that; it only changes how confidently we fill in that player.

The slot is already cut. Back in Step 1 the player table got a login_identity column, left empty for now. That single empty field is the entire hook for real login. Filling it in later is the whole migration — no new tables, no reshaped screens.

2You get the machinery for free

The reason this stays cheap is Step 2: Supabase ships a full auth system in the same box as the database. Email magic-links, and enterprise SSO (SAML / OIDC), are switches you turn on — not systems you build. So the “road” below is mostly configuration, plus one small step of linking a signed-in person to their player row.

3The three phases, made concrete

1
Pick your name Launch — today
No real login. On first open the app asks “Who are you?”; you tap your name from the roster and the phone remembers it (stored on that device). That remembered name fills in entered_by and comment authors. Anyone can log any match — the honour system, which is Option A. login_identity stays empty. This is exactly what the prototype already does.
2
Verify with a magic link The upgrade
Turn on Supabase email magic-link auth. You enter your email, click the link it sends, and you're signed in — no password. On that first sign-in we write your email into your player's login_identity, linking the account to the person. Now the API can tell a real claim from a wish: it can enforce “you may only log matches you played in,” which unlocks confirm-or-dispute (Option B). Pick-your-name doesn't disappear — the self-declared name simply becomes a verified one.
3
EF single sign-on End state
Point Supabase's SSO at EF's company identity provider. Now people sign in with their existing EF account — zero new passwords, profiles auto-provision on first login, and because only EF accounts can get in, the app is genuinely office-only (this is what finally replaces “reachable by URL” from Step 4). login_identity now holds the verified EF id. Same slot, stronger proof.

4Why none of it is a rebuild

Line the phases up and only the middle column ever moves:

PhaseWhat changesWhat stays exactly the same
1 → 2Add a sign-in screen; write login_identity on first sign-in; API starts checking identity on logMatch.Every table, every other screen, the ELO math, the feed, pick-your-name as the fallback label.
2 → 3Swap the magic-link provider for EF SSO in the Supabase dashboard.The login_identity link, the confirm/dispute flow, all seven tables, the whole frontend.
Decision: phase auth in, don't gate launch on it. Launch on Phase 1 (pick-your-name, already built). Move to Phase 2 when trust needs tightening — it's a config toggle plus the one-line link into login_identity. Adopt Phase 3 (EF SSO) when you want office-only access and auto-provisioned profiles. Each step is opt-in and reversible; none touches the data model or the screens.

5What to keep true at launch so the road stays open

Nothing to build now — just don't paint over the hooks that are already there:

  • Keep attributing authorship. Every comment stores its author_id, every match its entered_by — even though it's self-declared today.
  • Leave login_identity in place and empty. It's the single field Phase 2 fills.
  • Keep the status column on matches. Everything is confirmed now; it's what lets Phase 2 introduce pending/disputed as pure config.

All three already exist in the data model, so “keeping them true” costs nothing — it just means not stripping them out for looking unused at launch.

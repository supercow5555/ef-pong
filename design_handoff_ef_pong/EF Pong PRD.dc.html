EF Pong

A live, always-on leaderboard for office ping pong. Log the match you just played, watch your ELO move, settle rivalries, and talk a little trash — all on your phone and on the big screen in the office.

1Overview & vision

Ping pong in the office has turned competitive, and there's no shared source of truth for who's actually best. Wins are forgotten, bragging rights are contested, and there's no ladder to climb. EF Pong turns every casual game into a ranked match on a leaderboard everyone can see.

After a game, one (or both) of the players enters the result — e.g. "I beat Sofia 11–4." The system updates each player's ELO rating, re-orders the leaderboard, and posts the match to a public history feed where colleagues can react and comment. There's no bracket to manage: the season is one continuous live tournament, and rankings are always up to date.

Success looks like a leaderboard that people check daily, a wall display that draws a crowd, and enough friendly rivalry that people actually want to book the table.

2Goals & non-goals

Goals

  • Frictionless logging. Recording a match takes under 20 seconds, from a phone, right at the table.
  • A ranking people trust. ELO that reflects real skill, rewards beating stronger players, and accounts for how decisively you won.
  • A leaderboard worth watching. Clear ranks, ratings, records, and movement — readable on a phone and legible across the room on an office screen.
  • Raise the stakes. Rivalries, head-to-head records, "biggest upset" moments, reactions and trash talk that make wins feel like they matter.
  • Fresh starts. Quarterly seasons reset the ladder and enshrine each season's champion in a Hall of Fame.

Non-goals (for this version)

  • No tournament brackets, scheduling, or court/table booking.
  • No deep doubles support yet — singles is the core (doubles sketched in the Appendix).
  • No native mobile apps — a responsive web app covers phone + display.
  • No integrations with external rating bodies or public/company-wide social features beyond the office group.

6User stories

  • As a player, I can log a match by picking my opponent and entering the score, so the result counts toward our rankings.
  • As a player, I can see my current rank and ELO and how they changed after my last match.
  • As anyone in the office, I can open the leaderboard and instantly see who's #1 and how the top players stack up.
  • As a rival, I can view my head-to-head record against a specific person.
  • As a spectator, I can scroll the match feed and add a reaction or comment.
  • As a challenger, when I beat someone much higher-rated, my win is highlighted as an upset.
  • As a competitor, at season end I can see who's crowned champion and added to the Hall of Fame.
  • As an office, we can put the leaderboard on a wall screen that stays up to date on its own.

7Functional requirements

7.1  Leaderboard

The default screen. A ranked table of all active players for the current season, sorted by ELO (highest first).

Each row shows: rank number, player name + avatar, ELO score, win/loss record (e.g. 18–7), and a movement indicator (rank change and/or ELO change since a reference point, e.g. this week). The top 3 get visual emphasis (podium treatment: gold / silver / bronze accents).

  • Tapping a player opens their profile (§7.4).
  • Players with fewer than the provisional-game threshold (§8) are marked provisional and may be shown in a separate section or flagged, so an unproven 3–0 record doesn't sit above proven regulars unfairly.
  • Search/filter by name; a compact toggle for the wall-display mode (§10).

7.2  Log a match  Decided

The core action. Flow: choose winner → choose loser (opponent) → enter the score → submit. The system infers the winner from the higher score, validates the score, computes ELO changes, and updates the leaderboard immediately.

Match entry rules

  • A match is a single game to 11, win by 2 (so valid finals are 11–x where x≤9, or deuce games like 13–11).
  • Both players must be distinct and selectable from the roster; adding a new player should be quick (name only).
  • Timestamp is captured automatically; matches appear in the feed in reverse-chronological order.

Who confirms a result? — three options to choose from

You asked to explore this. All three assume logging is fast; they differ on how much we trust a single unverified entry. The right pick is coupled to the login decision (§9).

OptionHow it worksTrade-off
A. Open entryEither player logs the result; it counts immediately, no confirmation.Lowest friction; relies fully on the honor system. Fine for a small, trusting office. Easiest to build.
B. Confirm-or-dispute ← the target, post-loginThe person logging picks the opponent; the opponent gets a quick "confirm / dispute" prompt. Auto-confirms after a set window (e.g. 24h) so nothing gets stuck.Small friction, big trust gain. Catches typos and fake results. Requires a way to notify/identify the opponent.
C. Both agreeBoth players must be present/logged in and both tap confirm before it counts.Highest trust, highest friction — can stall if one player walks off. Best only if disputes become a real problem.

Decision: launch with Option A (open entry, no login), and move to Option B once login is in place. To make that a clean upgrade rather than a rebuild, the data model already gives every match a status field (confirmed / pending / disputed) — in Option A everything is simply confirmed on entry.

7.3  Match history feed & trash talk

A public, reverse-chronological feed of every recorded match. Each entry shows the two players, the score, the winner, each player's ELO change (e.g. +22 / −22), the time, and any "upset" badge (§7.6).

Before login exists (Phase 1, §9), the social layer still works — it just needs a name to attach to a comment:

  • Reactions are anonymous. One-tap reactions only need a count (“12 🔥”), so they work with no identity at all. We don't stop repeat taps or show who reacted — fine for a fun office tool.
  • Comments use “pick-your-name”. On first open, the app asks “Who are you?” and the person taps their name from the roster; it's remembered on that device and attributed to every comment they post. It's the honour system (someone could pick a name that isn't theirs), which is acceptable at our scale and upgrades cleanly — the data model already stores an author per comment, so Phase 2 login simply replaces the self-declared name with a verified one.
  • Reactions: one-tap emoji-style reactions on any match (the crowd's response — 🔥, 😮, 🤝, etc.).
  • Comments: a short threaded comment section per match for trash talk and banter.
  • Light moderation: authors can delete their own comments, and a single admin (the product owner) can remove anything if it goes too far. Keep it civil-ish, but this is the stakes engine — don't over-police.

7.4  Player profile & head-to-head

Per player: current rank, ELO (with a small trend line over the season), W/L record, current streak, recent matches, and biggest wins.

  • Head-to-head: pick any other player to see the lifetime record between you (e.g. "You lead Marco 6–4"), plus your last few meetings. This is the rivalry surface.
  • Rival highlight: optionally surface a player's closest/most-played rival on their profile.

7.5  Seasons & Hall of Fame

  • The ladder runs in quarterly seasons. At season end, standings are frozen and the champion (and podium) are recorded.
  • Hard reset (decided): at the start of each season, every player's rating returns to 1000 and the provisional period (§8) begins again. Simple, clean, and gives everyone a genuine fresh shot at the top. Past seasons live on in the Hall of Fame. (A softer reset could be revisited later if the ladder feels too "reset-happy".)
  • A Hall of Fame page lists past season champions and notable records (highest peak ELO, longest streak, biggest single upset).

7.6  Biggest upset & biggest mover

  • An upset is a win by a lower-rated player over a much higher-rated one (threshold by rating gap, e.g. 150+ points). Upset wins get a badge in the feed and can headline the leaderboard/display.
  • Biggest mover highlights the player who gained the most ELO in a period (day/week) — a reason for spectators to check back.

8Ranking system (ELO)

Ratings use a standard ELO model, adapted so that the margin of victory matters (per your call) — an 11–9 nail-biter should move the needle less than an 11–1 blowout.

Core formula

Every player starts at 1000. For a match between A and B, A's expected result is:

EA = 1 / (1 + 10((RB − RA) / 400))

After the match, A's rating changes by:

ΔRA = K × M × (SA − EA)

where SA = 1 if A won, 0 if A lost. B changes by the exact opposite (ΔRB = −ΔRA), so ratings are zero-sum — points are only ever transferred, never created.

ParameterProposed valueNotes
Starting rating1000Everyone begins equal
K (base)24How fast ratings move. Higher = more volatile
K (provisional)40 for first 5 gamesNew players calibrate faster, then settle
Margin multiplier M1 + ln(diff) / ln(11)diff = winner points − loser points. Ranges 1.0 (win by 1) → 2.0 (11–0)

Worked example

A (1000) beats B (1000) by 11–4 (diff = 7).

  • Expected: EA = 1 / (1 + 100) = 0.50 (evenly matched).
  • Margin: M = 1 + ln(7)/ln(11) = 1.81.
  • Change: ΔRA = 24 × 1.81 × (1 − 0.50) ≈ +22. So A → 1022, B → 978.

Beating a much stronger player yields a bigger jump (upset); losing to a much weaker one costs more. All the constants above are tunable — we can calibrate K and the margin curve after a few weeks of real games. This math runs on the server, inside the logMatch operation, so every client agrees and no result can be faked (see Talking To It, §3).

Edge cases & rules to decide

  • Deuce games (e.g. 13–11) always have diff = 2 → M ≈ 1.29. Reasonable.
  • Best-of-N (playing 3 or 5 games) is out of the launch — single games only. Parked in the backlog; if people start playing full matches, we'll add a match mode later.
  • Rating floor (never drop below, say, 100)? Recommended to avoid runaway losses.
  • Inactivity: do stale ratings decay or freeze between seasons? (See §7.5 reset.)

10Platforms & surfaces

  • Phone (primary input): a responsive web app optimized for logging a match in seconds right at the table. Large tap targets (≥44px), minimal typing.
  • Office wall display (primary output): an always-on, glanceable leaderboard mode — big type, top players, live match ticker, current champion, biggest mover. Auto-refreshes; readable across the room; no interaction required.
  • Desktop: the same web app, comfortable for browsing profiles and history.

One responsive codebase serves all three; the display is a dedicated full-screen "kiosk" view of the same data.

11Data model — in plain terms

New to databases? Think of a database as a set of spreadsheets ("tables"). Each table has columns (the fields) and rows (one record each). Tables link by referencing each other's IDs — a match "points at" the two players in it. That's the whole idea. We'll go deeper together in the architecture discussion; this is just what we need to store.

TableWhat each row holds
Playerid, name, avatar, join date, (later) linked login identity
Standingsid, player (→Player), season (→Season), current ELO, wins, losses, peak — one row per player per season; the leaderboard, cached. Added to handle the quarterly reset cleanly (past seasons stay frozen = Hall of Fame)
Matchid, winner (→Player), loser (→Player), winner score, loser score, ELO change, timestamp, season, entered-by, status (confirmed/pending/disputed)
RatingHistoryid, player (→Player), match (→Match), rating after, timestamp — powers the trend line and "biggest mover"
Seasonid, name, start date, end date, champion (→Player), active flag
Reactionid, match (→Match), player (→Player), type — the one-tap responses
Commentid, match (→Match), author (→Player), text, timestamp — the trash talk

The Standings table is the one deliberately-cached exception — a fast copy of each player's current ELO/record for the leaderboard, rebuildable from matches. Everything else (rank order, head-to-head, upsets) is computed from these tables and never stored. See EF Pong Data Model for every column, type, and relationship drawn out.

12Non-functional requirements

  • Speed: leaderboard and feed load in about a second; the display refreshes without a visible reload.
  • Correctness: ELO is computed deterministically and consistently; a match can be corrected/voided by an admin with ratings recalculated.
  • Availability: it's an office toy, but the wall display should stay up for days unattended.
  • Access: office-only. In Phase 1, obscurity + trust; from Phase 2, gated by login.
  • Privacy: only office colleagues; no public exposure of names/records outside the group.
  • Data integrity: nothing is silently lost; disputes and corrections are auditable.

15Appendix — future ideas

  • Doubles: log 2v2 games. Simplest approach: treat a team's rating as the average of its two players, distribute the ELO change to each. Keep a separate doubles rating so it doesn't muddy singles. (Deliberately deferred to keep MVP scoring clean.)
  • Achievements & titles: "Giant Slayer", "On Fire" (win streaks), "Nemesis" (owns a specific rival).
  • Rivalry pages & taunts: challenge someone; pre-match trash talk.
  • Notifications: "You've been overtaken", "Your record was just logged", weekly recap.
  • Stats: peak ELO, favorite/nightmare opponents, time-of-day performance.
